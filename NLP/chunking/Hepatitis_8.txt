Hepatitis Hepatitis Unlike hepatitis B, most cases of hepatitis C lead to chronic infection.  Hepatitis C is the second most common cause of cirrhosis in the US .  In the 1970s and 1980s, blood transfusions were a major factor in spreading hepatitis C virus.  Since widespread screening of blood products for hepatitis C began in 1992, the risk of acquiring hepatitis C from a blood transfusion has decreased from approximately 10% in the 1970s to 1 in 2 million currently. Parasites can also infect the liver and activate the immune response, resulting in symptoms of acute hepatitis with increased serum IgE .  Of the protozoans, Trypanosoma cruzi, Leishmania species, and the malaria-causing Plasmodium species all can cause liver inflammation.
